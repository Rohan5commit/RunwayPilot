# Problem Statement

Startups, student founders, and SMB operators rarely have a real-time operating view of their cash position. The information exists, but it is scattered across spreadsheets, card statements, accounting exports, subscription invoices, payroll runs, and ad platforms. By the time someone notices that runway is shrinking or spend quality is deteriorating, the problem is already costly.

## The financial visibility problem

Founders usually track revenue, burn, and balance through periodic spreadsheet updates. That approach breaks down when spending behavior changes mid-month. A surprise infrastructure charge, duplicated tool subscription, contractor creep, or slowing receivable cycle can compress runway long before the next planning session.

## Who it affects

- student founders managing their first startup budget
- indie hackers trying to stay default alive
- startup operators juggling growth and cost control
- finance leads at early-stage companies without full FP&A tooling
- small business owners with limited time and fragmented systems
- accelerators that want earlier warning across portfolio companies

## Why existing workflows fail

Traditional workflows fail because they are manual, lagging, and hard to simulate. Most teams can report what happened last month, but not what the current trend means for the next three to six months. They also struggle to connect individual transactions to operational risk: duplicate subscriptions, vendor concentration, recurring waste, or unexplained spikes.

## Why early warning matters

Earlier intervention changes outcomes. If a founder sees runway compression four to eight weeks earlier, they can renegotiate vendors, reduce waste, slow hiring, reprice offerings, or improve collections before the business enters emergency mode. Early warning is not just a finance feature. It is an operating advantage.
